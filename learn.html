The Science Behind the Data

Understanding the physical and environmental factors that create climate vulnerability across NYC neighborhoods.

The Heat Island Effect

Central Brooklyn summers run 7°F hotter than tree-lined wealthy areas. This isn't natural variation—it's the result of different built environments:

  • Surface materials: Concrete and asphalt absorb and retain heat
  • Building density: Tall buildings without setbacks trap heat
  • Lack of vegetation: Fewer trees means less cooling through evapotranspiration
  • Air conditioning: Outdoor units add heat to streets while cooling indoor spaces

Redlining's Temperature Legacy

Formerly D-graded neighborhoods average 1.6°F above city temperatures, while formerly A-graded areas remain 4.2°F cooler. The same areas denied investment in the 1930s face the highest heat risk today.

Flood Risk Patterns

Flooding in NYC isn't just about proximity to water—it's about infrastructure capacity and neighborhood investment:

  • Impermeable surfaces: Concrete prevents water absorption, increasing runoff
  • Aging infrastructure: Older sewer systems can't handle increased rainfall
  • Lack of green infrastructure: Missing bioswales and rain gardens that absorb stormwater
  • Basement housing: Vulnerable populations in flood-prone illegal units

Green Infrastructure Benefits

Natural and nature-based solutions that provide multiple community benefits:

  • Trees: A single mature tree can cool the equivalent of 5 air conditioners
  • Bioswales: Capture stormwater while reducing local temperatures by 2-3°F
  • Green roofs: Reduce building energy costs and surrounding air temperatures by up to 5°F
  • Parks and gardens: Provide community space while improving air quality

Historical Context

Today's climate vulnerability patterns trace directly back to discriminatory policies that shaped NYC's development.

Redlining Legacy

1930s-1960s

Federal Housing Discrimination

Redlining systematically denied investment to neighborhoods with Black and Latino residents, limiting access to homeownership and neighborhood improvements.

1950s-1970s

Highway Construction

Interstate highways routed through communities of color, increasing air pollution and creating heat-absorbing concrete corridors.

1980s-Present

Environmental Racism

Concentration of polluting facilities, waste sites, and industrial infrastructure in low-income communities and communities of color.

The Connection to Climate

Climate vulnerability isn't separate from housing discrimination, environmental racism, or economic inequality—it's the result of these systems. Understanding this history is essential to creating solutions that address root causes, not just symptoms.
